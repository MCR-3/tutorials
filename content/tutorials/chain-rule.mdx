Every training step in every DL framework — PyTorch, JAX, simplegrad — ultimately reduces to one rule from calculus: **the chain rule**.

Here it is. If $z$ depends on $y$, and $y$ depends on $x$, then:

$$
\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial z}{\partial y} \cdot \frac{\partial y}{\partial x}
$$

That's it. That's the engine. Everything else is application.

## What It Means

The chain rule tells you how to compute the gradient of a composed function. If you have a sequence of operations, you can find how a change in the input affects the output by multiplying the local derivatives at each step.

Think of a two-operation pipeline:

$$
x \xrightarrow{f} y \xrightarrow{g} z
$$

The derivative of the final output $z$ with respect to the initial input $x$ is:

$$
\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial z}{\partial y} \cdot \frac{\partial y}{\partial x}
= g'(y) \cdot f'(x)
$$

You multiply the derivatives together, right to left.

## A Concrete Example

Let's say:

$$
f(x) = x^2, \quad g(y) = 3y + 1
$$

So:

$$
z = g(f(x)) = 3x^2 + 1
$$

The derivatives are:

$$
\frac{\partial y}{\partial x} = f'(x) = 2x, \qquad \frac{\partial z}{\partial y} = g'(y) = 3
$$

Applying the chain rule:

$$
\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = 3 \cdot 2x = 6x
$$

Which matches what you'd get differentiating $3x^2 + 1$ directly.

## Why This Matters for Neural Networks

A neural network is a composition of functions. A simple two-layer network looks like:

$$
\hat{y} = \sigma\bigl(W_2 \cdot \sigma(W_1 \cdot x + b_1) + b_2\bigr)
$$

where $\sigma$ is some activation function (like ReLU). Training means finding weights $W_1, W_2, b_1, b_2$ that minimize some loss $\mathcal{L}(\hat{y}, y)$.

To do that with gradient descent, we need:

$$
\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial W_1}, \quad \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial W_2}, \quad \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial b_1}, \quad \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial b_2}
$$

The chain rule gives us all of them. We start from the loss, move backwards through the network, and at each layer we multiply by the local derivative. This is **backpropagation** — it's the chain rule, applied recursively.

## The General Form

For a chain of $n$ functions $f_1, f_2, \ldots, f_n$:

$$
\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial h_n} \cdot \frac{\partial h_n}{\partial h_{n-1}} \cdots \frac{\partial h_2}{\partial h_1} \cdot \frac{\partial h_1}{\partial x}
$$

Each $\frac{\partial h_{k+1}}{\partial h_k}$ is a **local gradient** — the derivative of that specific operation. The autograd engine computes these automatically and accumulates them.

In simplegrad, every operation on a `Tensor` stores its own backward function. When you call `.backward()`, the engine walks the computation graph in reverse and applies exactly this formula.

## Seeing It Run

Here's the minimal example. A 2-element input, a weight matrix, a mean reduction, then `.backward()`. After that, `x.grad` holds $\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}$ — computed entirely via the chain rule:

```simplegrad
import simplegrad as sg

x = sg.Tensor([[1.0, 2.0]])
w = sg.Tensor([[0.5], [-0.5]])
y = sg.mean(x @ w)
y.backward()

print(x.grad)  # [[0.25, -0.25]]
```

```pytorch
import torch

x = torch.tensor([[1.0, 2.0]], requires_grad=True)
w = torch.tensor([[0.5], [-0.5]], requires_grad=True)
y = (x @ w).mean()
y.backward()

print(x.grad)  # tensor([[0.2500, -0.2500]])
```
